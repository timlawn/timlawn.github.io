[cite_start]Some individuals maintain cognitive function despite substantial Alzheimer’s disease (AD) pathology. [cite: 10] [cite_start]The structural integrity, activity, and metabolism of the locus coeruleus (LC) have been associated with cognitive resilience. [cite: 11] [cite_start]The LC provides widespread neuromodulatory control over networks subserving critical cognitive functions like attention and memory, including the frontoparietal control network that has also previously been associated with resilience. [cite: 12] [cite_start]However, whether the LC’s protective effects on cognition are conferred through its modulation of these large-scale brain networks remains untested. [cite: 13]

[cite_start]Here, we examined 393 cognitively unimpaired, amyloid-positive individuals from the A4 study using linear mixed-effects models to investigate whether seed-based LC functional connectivity (LC-FC) moderates amyloid-related cognitive decline. [cite: 14] [cite_start]Higher baseline LC-FC was associated with attenuated cognitive decline over 5.5 years, driven primarily by connectivity to frontoparietal control, attentional, and somatomotor networks. [cite: 15] [cite_start]Critically, this protection was most pronounced in individuals with greater amyloid burden, ε4+ carriers, and particularly female ε4+ carriers. [cite: 16] [cite_start]These findings suggest that LC-mediated cognitive resilience operates through modulation of distributed brain networks and indicate that noradrenergic interventions may be most beneficial when targeted to high-risk individuals exhibiting lower LC connectivity. [cite: 17]
